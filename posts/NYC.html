Exploring my new neighborhood
(The importance of community)

I spent three weeks building what I thought was a beautiful analytics dashboard. It had everything. Nobody opened it after week one. Here's what went wrong.

✦

It was the most beautiful dashboard I had ever built. Nested filters. Drill-down charts. Color-coded KPI tiles. A date range selector that actually worked properly. I was proud of it in the way you're proud of something you've poured real time into.

I presented it in the Thursday all-hands. There were nods. Someone asked if they could also get the data in a spreadsheet. I said "sure, but the dashboard is actually richer." They nodded again.

By week two, I noticed the usage stats. Eleven views total. Six of them were mine, checking that it loaded correctly.

What I Thought the Problem Was

My first instinct was that people just weren't comfortable with dashboards. "Digital transformation takes time," I told myself. I added a short instructional video. I sent a Slack message walking through the features. I updated the color scheme because someone had mentioned it "felt a bit cold."

None of this helped.

"A dashboard nobody uses is not a dashboard. It's a very expensive screenshot."

What the Problem Actually Was

The turning point came when I sat down with the marketing lead — the person I had nominally built this for — and asked her to walk me through her Monday morning. What did she actually do with data? What questions did she need answered?

Her answers were humbling. She needed three numbers every Monday: last week's conversion rate, how that compared to the prior week, and whether it was above or below her quarterly target. That was it. The decision she made with those three numbers took about thirty seconds.

My dashboard gave her forty charts and required about twenty clicks to surface those three numbers.

What I Did Differently the Second Time

I rebuilt it in an afternoon. One page. Three numbers, big and clear at the top. A simple week-over-week sparkline. A single sentence of context written in plain English — not "WoW Δ +4.2%" but "Conversion rate improved 4.2% from last week, now 0.3% above your Q4 target."

She opened it every Monday for the next six months without me ever reminding her.

The Lesson I'm Still Learning

The goal of a dashboard isn't to display data. It's to support a decision. And the best way to know what decision someone is making is to ask them — not at the end when you're ready to present, but at the very beginning, before you've opened Tableau.

I now start every analytics project with the same question: "What will you do differently based on what this tells you?" If the person can't answer that, we're not ready to build anything yet.

It's a slower start. But it's almost never three wasted weeks.
